Blockchain is a digital ledger used to store data. The data is stored in a decentralized database, spread across a network of computers known as nodes (Allena, 1062). When new blocks of data are added, they are verified through a method known as proof of work. After verification, each node in the network is updated to reflect the new data (Rodeck and Schmidt).
Permissionless blockchains are blockchains that anyone can use, and grant users anonymity and security (Allena, 1067). In contrast, permissioned blockchains are more centralized, and trade anonymity for increased efficiency and decreased energy consumption (Allena, 1067).

Bitcoin is a cryptocurrency built on blockchain. The phrase “mining Bitcoin” describes how people can earn Bitcoins. Being one of the nodes in the blockchain network to verify the addition of new data will earn the user a certain number of Bitcoins (Lavie). In order for Bitcoins to keep their value as more blocks are added, Bitcoin halving occurs, in which the value of each new Bitcoin is cut in half (Lavie). The current market value of Bitcoin is approximately 44,000 USD per single Bitcoin (CITE).

Cryptocurrencies are decentralized digital currencies, such as Bitcoin or Ethereum. Cryptocurrencies can be seen as an investment, or used as legitimate currency at places that accept it (Ashford and Schmidt).

In blockchains, hash functions are used to verify the legitimacy of data. Generally, they are one way of encrypting data by using a mathematical function to transform some given information into some other unrecognizable form. Hashing algorithms are commonly used with passwords for security, so that the hashed form of a password is stored in a database, rather than the password itself. Blockchain hash functions are complex algorithms that take large amounts of computing power to calculate (Allena, 1065).

Miners are people who participate in the blockchain network via their own hardware.

Non-fungible tokens, or NFTs, are unique, immutable pieces of data generally associated with some sort of digital media (Clark). Like cryptocurrencies, they are also supported by blockchains. They are often seen as digital collector's items, and sell for ridiculously high prices on the Internet (Clark).

Proof of work is the method used to verify blockchain data. It involves multiple nodes calculating complicated hashes in order to make sure transactions are legitimate (Chandler).
